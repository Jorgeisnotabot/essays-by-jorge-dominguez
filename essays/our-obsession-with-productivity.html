Our Obsession with Productivity

June 2025 • 6 min read

We have become extremely inward-facing, somehow obsessed with the future, as if we are still teenagers trapped in adult bodies. We learned to wait for desire, but perhaps we learned this lesson too well. Now we find ourselves caught in a strange trap of our own making.

Reading The Stranger by Albert Camus recently, I cannot avoid thinking that life will have only the meaning we give it. Yet here we are, creating elaborate systems to measure and optimize our existence, building what feels like a messy dashboard to navigate life—too many dimensions, too many metrics, all pulling us away from the simple fact of being alive right now.

We have created a mythology of cosmic bookkeeping, believing that if we delay happiness now, we will somehow earn a better outcome later. We live in constant fear that if we stop to enjoy something, we will miss a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity. But we could postpone happiness all we want, and still no magic force in the universe will deliver us a present. There is no external force taking note of our sacrifices—it's just us.

The truth is, we are estranged from our own senses. We are an extremely well-designed system for sensing, yet we have become trapped in an internal monologue that keeps us waiting and delaying. This is perhaps why we believe in scarcity, why we think opportunities are rare and must be earned through suffering.

Our sensory apparatus is sophisticated beyond measure, capable of finding richness in any moment. But we live almost entirely in the commentary track, in the voice that tells us about better moments coming or the ones we just missed. We have learned to trust our internal productivity manager more than the direct experience of being alive.

Maybe we should learn not to be so obsessed with retrospection and evaluation of every aspect we encounter in our lives. Life is not a problem to be optimized but a reality to be experienced. The meaning is not waiting for us in some future moment of achievement—it is here, in whatever we choose to make of right now.
